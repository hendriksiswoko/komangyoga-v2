Nusa Penida

Kelingking Beach: Bali's most photographed cliff, explained

MyBroBali Travel Guide · 6 min read

If you've seen one photo of Bali on social media, there's a good chance it was this one: a T-Rex-shaped cliff dropping into turquoise water, with a narrow strip of white sand curving below. That's Kelingking Beach, on the west coast of Nusa Penida, and it's become the island's single most photographed viewpoint.

The viewpoint versus the beach

Most visitors never actually reach the sand. The main viewpoint — a small hill with a couple of warungs and a fenced lookout — already gives you the classic shot, and it requires no hiking at all beyond a short walk from the parking area.

Reaching the beach itself is a different story. The path down is steep, narrow, and partly makeshift, with sections of stairs, dirt track, and rope-assisted scrambling. It takes 30–45 minutes down and considerably longer back up in the heat. It's doable for reasonably fit visitors with proper shoes, but it's not for everyone, and it's not something to attempt in flip-flops or after rain.

Is it worth the climb?

If you're comfortable with a strenuous, non-technical hike, the beach at the bottom is genuinely worth it — calm water, dramatic cliff walls on both sides, and far fewer people than the viewpoint above. If you're not up for it, the viewpoint alone is still one of the best panoramas in Bali and takes only minutes to reach.

Good to know before you go

  • Nusa Penida is a separate island — getting there requires a speedboat crossing from Sanur, Padang Bai, or Kusamba (30–45 minutes)
  • Kelingking is usually combined with Angel's Billabong, Broken Beach, and Crystal Bay in a full-day island tour
  • Mornings tend to have clearer light and smaller crowds at the viewpoint
  • Bring cash for parking and small entrance fees, and wear proper shoes if you're descending to the beach

Getting there

Because Nusa Penida requires a boat crossing and the island's roads are rougher than mainland Bali, most visitors book it as a full-day trip with transport arranged on both sides — car to the harbor, boat across, then a driver waiting on Nusa Penida for the day.
